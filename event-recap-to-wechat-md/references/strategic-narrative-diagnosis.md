# Strategic Narrative Diagnosis

Use this reference before drafting event articles for a recurring partner, community, brand, institution, or ecosystem organization. The goal is to identify what the organization needs the event to prove, not only what happened at the event.

## Core Frame

An event article is often a strategic narrative surface.

It should answer:

- Why does this organization appear in this scene?
- What role does it want to be understood as playing?
- What evidence from the event supports that role?
- How does the article fit the organization's existing language and public history?
- What should readers understand differently after reading?

Do not reduce this to "write more strategically." The diagnosis must be tied to evidence, language, audience, and public boundaries.

## Strategic Value Types

Choose one primary value and at most two secondary values.

| Value | What the article needs to prove |
| --- | --- |
| Ecosystem convening | The organization can bring together relevant people, communities, partners, developers, customers, or institutions. |
| Industry credibility | The organization has earned a place in this topic, sector, or technical scene. |
| Developer / learner connection | The organization connects learning, practice, projects, tools, and real-world industry scenes. |
| Partner relationship | The event maintains or deepens partner trust and makes partner contributions visible. |
| Brand position | The event reinforces the way the organization wants to be remembered. |
| Conversion / participation | The reader should register, attend, join, follow, apply, or continue a conversation. |
| Public knowledge | The event produced questions, observations, methods, or cases worth preserving. |
| Internal alignment | The article helps the team understand what the event meant and how to continue. |

## Organization Role

Do not assume every organization is the host.

Identify the role:

- host
- co-host
- supporting unit
- content partner
- community partner
- ecosystem connector
- developer community
- learning platform
- technical partner
- exhibitor
- forum organizer
- attendee / observer

Use careful wording when the role is not confirmed:

- "参与"
- "共同支持"
- "在现场与...交流"
- "作为...的一部分"
- "连接了..."

Avoid turning:

- support into hosting
- attendance into initiation
- intention into confirmed collaboration
- internal relationship into public endorsement

## Style Knowledge Base

For partner-specific work, build or request a small style knowledge base before drafting.

Useful sources:

- WeChat historical event reports
- event announcements
- post-event recaps
- partner announcements
- founder / team / community articles
- project pages
- website copy
- registration pages
- official posters and agendas

Extract patterns, not sentences.

| Dimension | What to extract |
| --- | --- |
| Titles | Question titles, invitation titles, outcome titles, emotional titles, technical titles. |
| Openings | Trend, scene, person, public question, organizational mission, or event fact. |
| Reader relationship | Learners, developers, founders, partners, customers, educators, community members, general followers. |
| Recurring themes | Open source, learning, developer ecosystem, industry adoption, youth growth, community connection, technical infrastructure. |
| Tone | restrained, warm, official, technical, community-like, invitational, analytical. |
| Evidence style | How the organization uses numbers, cases, partner names, quotes, photos, and project examples. |
| CTA | register, attend, scan code, join community, follow project, continue talking, visit booth. |
| Avoid | overused slogans, exaggerated claims, AI-flavored summaries, phrases already used by another public article. |

The knowledge base is not a copy bank. It is a style and positioning reference.

## Public Content Check

When public reports or historical articles exist, compare them before drafting.

Ask:

- What facts are already public?
- What angle has already been used?
- Which phrases are occupied by official or partner copy?
- What can this organization uniquely say?
- What claims need approval?
- What does the new article add beyond repetition?

- Recommended writing move:

## Four Gates

Before drafting and before finalizing, pass four gates.

| Gate | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Strategic gate | Does the article serve the organization's actual strategic value? |
| Style gate | Does it fit the organization's historical language and reader relationship? |
| Scene gate | Does it contain real event evidence: people, questions, interactions, artifacts, or turning points? |
| Boundary gate | Does it avoid overclaiming facts, roles, relationships, permissions, and private context? |

Common failures:

- Passing the style gate only: the article feels on-brand but empty.
- Passing the scene gate only: the article has texture but does not sound like the organization.
- Passing the strategic gate only: the article becomes slogans.
- Passing no boundary gate: the article may create relationship or approval problems.

## Usage Guidance For Future Users

When using this skill, future users should provide:

1. The event materials: agenda, notes, transcript, photos, links, public reports.
2. The organization context: role in the event, audience, goal, public boundaries.
3. Historical style references: 3-5 past articles or WeChat links that represent the desired voice.
4. The desired output: invitation article, forum preview, recap, partner proof, memory pack, or content asset pack.
5. The approval constraints: what names, claims, quotes, images, and partner relationships need confirmation.

If the user cannot provide historical style references, first produce a neutral article direction card and ask for style samples before drafting a final partner-specific piece.
